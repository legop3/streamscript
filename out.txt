The blue color of the sky is primarily due to a phenomenon called **Rayleigh scattering**, which involves the interaction of sunlight with Earth's atmosphere. Here's a step-by-step explanation:

1. **Sunlight Composition**: Sunlight appears white but is actually composed of all the visible wavelengths of light, from violet (shortest wavelength) to red (longest wavelength).

2. **Atmospheric Scattering**: When sunlight enters Earth's atmosphere, it collides with molecules and small particles in the air (primarily nitrogen and oxygen). These interactions cause the light to scatter in different directions.

3. **Wavelength-Dependent Scattering**: Shorter wavelengths (blue/violet) are scattered more efficiently than longer wavelengths (red/orange) because they interact more strongly with the tiny molecules in the atmosphere. This is known as **Rayleigh scattering**, named after Lord Rayleigh, who first described it.

4. **Human Perception**: While violet light scatters even more than blue, our eyes are less sensitive to violet and there is relatively less violet light in sunlight compared to blue. Additionally, some of the scattered violet light is absorbed by the upper atmosphere. As a result, we perceive the sky as predominantly blue.

5. **Sunrise/Sunset Effect**: During sunrise or sunset, sunlight travels through more of Earth's atmosphere. The longer path scatters out most of the blue and green wavelengths, leaving the longer red and orange wavelengths to dominate, which is why the sky appears reddish or orange at these times.

In summary, the sky appears blue because shorter wavelengths of light (blue) are scattered more by atmospheric molecules, and our eyes are better adapted to detect this wavelength. This effect is a combination of physics (Rayleigh scattering) and biology (human vision).
